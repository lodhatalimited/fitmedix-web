Knowing how to dress a wound properly can make a real difference to how quickly it heals — and whether it gets infected. Whether you're dealing with a minor cut, a post-surgical wound, or a more stubborn abrasion, the principles are the same.

This guide walks you through everything you need to know, step by step.

## What You'll Need

Before you start, gather the following:

- Clean gloves (disposable, non-latex if possible)
- Sterile saline solution or clean running water
- A sterile wound dressing appropriate for the wound size
- Medical tape (if your dressing is not self-adhesive)
- Scissors (sterile if possible)
- A clean, flat surface to work on

Having everything ready before you begin means you won't need to stop mid-way and risk contamination.

## Step 1: Wash Your Hands Thoroughly

This is the single most important step. Before touching any wound, wash your hands with soap and water for at least 20 seconds. If soap and water aren't available, use hand sanitiser with at least 60% alcohol.

Put on your clean gloves before handling any wound dressing materials.

## Step 2: Clean the Wound

Gently rinse the wound under cool, clean running water for 5–10 minutes to remove dirt and debris. Avoid using cotton wool directly on the wound as fibres can get stuck.

If the wound is on a limb, you can hold it under the tap. For wounds on the torso, use a sterile saline wipe or pour saline solution gently over the area.

**Do not use:**
- Hydrogen peroxide
- Iodine
- Alcohol directly on the wound

These can damage healthy tissue and slow healing.

## Step 3: Pat Dry

Using a clean, lint-free cloth or sterile gauze swab, gently pat the area around the wound dry. Do not rub. The wound should be dry before you apply the dressing — moisture trapped under a dressing can encourage bacterial growth.

## Step 4: Choose the Right Dressing

Choosing the right size and type of dressing is critical. The dressing should:

- Cover the entire wound with at least 1–2cm overlap on all sides
- Be appropriate for the wound type (dry wound vs. wet/exuding wound)
- Be sterile and individually packaged

For most everyday wounds, an adhesive wound dressing (also called an island dressing) is ideal. These have a central absorbent pad surrounded by an adhesive border, making them easy to apply and secure without tape.

**Common sizes:**
- **6cm x 7cm** — fingertip cuts, small abrasions
- **8cm x 10cm** — larger cuts, minor lacerations
- **10cm x 10cm** — surgical wounds, deeper cuts
- **10cm x 15cm (or 8cm x 15mm)** — larger wounds, limb injuries
- **10cm x 20cm** — extensive wounds or dressings needed to wrap around joints

## Step 5: Apply the Dressing

Peel back the packaging without touching the central sterile pad. Position the dressing over the wound and press firmly around the edges to seal.

If using a non-adhesive dressing, centre it over the wound and secure the edges with medical tape, making sure there are no gaps.

**Tips:**
- Smooth out any creases — air pockets can breed bacteria
- For wounds near joints, apply the dressing so it won't be pulled when the joint moves
- For deeper or heavily bleeding wounds, seek medical attention

## Step 6: Label and Note the Date

It sounds simple but it makes a real difference: write the date of dressing application on the packaging or on a piece of tape attached nearby. This helps you track how long the dressing has been on.

## How Often Should You Change the Dressing?

For most minor wounds, change the dressing every 1–2 days, or sooner if it becomes wet, dirty, or starts to lift at the edges.

For surgical or more serious wounds, follow your healthcare provider's specific guidance. Changing dressings too frequently can disturb the healing tissue, while leaving them too long risks infection.

## Signs You Should See a Doctor

Seek medical attention if you notice any of the following:

- Increasing redness, warmth, or swelling around the wound
- Pus or unusual discharge
- The wound edges are not coming together
- Fever or feeling unwell
- The wound is deeper than it looks or won't stop bleeding after 10 minutes of pressure
