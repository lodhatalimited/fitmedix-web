Walk into any pharmacy or search online and you'll find wound dressings in dozens of sizes, shapes, and types. For something that seems simple, the choice can feel overwhelming.

This guide cuts through the confusion. By the end, you'll know exactly which dressing to reach for — whatever the wound.

## Understanding Wound Dressing Types

Not all dressings are the same. Before choosing a size, it helps to understand the main types:

### Adhesive Island Dressings
The most common type for everyday use. A central non-adherent absorbent pad is surrounded by a self-adhesive border. No tape required. Ideal for:
- Post-surgical wounds
- Cuts and lacerations
- Abrasions and grazes
- Blisters

### Transparent Film Dressings
A thin, clear, waterproof film with no absorbent pad. Lets you monitor the wound without removing the dressing. Ideal for:
- Superficial wounds with little to no fluid
- IV catheter sites
- Skin tears
- Protecting at-risk skin

### Gauze Swabs
Woven or non-woven squares of gauze. Highly absorbent and versatile. Used for:
- Cleaning and debriding wounds
- Packing larger wounds
- General wound management in clinical settings

### Foam Dressings
Thicker, highly absorbent dressings for wounds producing significant fluid. Less common for home use.

## Choosing the Right Size

The golden rule: **the dressing should cover the entire wound with at least 1–2cm of overlap on every side.**

A dressing that's too small won't protect the edges of the wound. One that's too large will fold and create pressure points. Here's a practical guide:

| Wound size | Recommended dressing | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| Under 2cm | 6cm x 7cm | Fingertip cuts, small grazes |
| 2–4cm | 8cm x 6cm | Medium cuts, minor lacerations |
| 4–6cm | 10cm x 8cm | Larger cuts, hand or foot wounds |
| 6–8cm | 10cm x 10cm | Surgical wounds, deeper cuts |
| 8–12cm | 10cm x 15cm or 8cm x 15cm | Limb wounds, post-op sites |
| Over 12cm | 10cm x 20cm | Extensive wounds, joint coverage |

When in doubt, go one size up.

## Matching the Dressing to the Situation

### Minor cuts at home
An adhesive island dressing in the right size is all you need. Keep a few different sizes in your first aid kit — a pack of assorted sizes is ideal.

### Post-surgical wounds
Your surgeon will advise on the specific dressing type. For most post-op care at home, adhesive island dressings are standard. Change as directed — usually every 1–2 days.

### Children's wounds
Use a smaller dressing with a soft, gentle adhesive. Avoid dressings that are significantly larger than the wound as they can pull at delicate skin on removal.

### Wounds on joints (knees, elbows, knuckles)
Use a larger dressing than the wound requires — joints flex and pull at the dressing edges. A 10cm x 15cm dressing on a knee, for example, gives far better coverage than a tight-fitting 6cm x 7cm one.

### Weeping or heavily exuding wounds
Choose a dressing with a higher absorbency pad. Adhesive island dressings work well for moderate exudate. If the dressing is soaking through within a few hours, the wound may need clinical assessment.

### Waterproof protection
If the patient needs to shower or is in a humid environment, a transparent film dressing over the top of the primary dressing provides a waterproof barrier.

## When to Seek Professional Help

No dressing will fix a wound that needs medical attention. See a doctor or go to A&E if:

- The wound is deep (you can see fat, muscle, or bone)
- Bleeding doesn't stop after 10 minutes of firm pressure
- The wound is from a bite (human or animal)
- There are signs of infection: increased pain, redness, swelling, pus, or fever
- The wound is on the face and may need closure for cosmetic reasons
