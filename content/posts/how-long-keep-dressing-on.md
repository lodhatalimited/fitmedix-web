It's a question almost everyone has asked at some point: how long is too long to keep a wound dressing on? And when is changing it too often actually doing more harm than good?

The answer depends on the type of wound and the dressing — but there are clear, practical guidelines you can follow.

## The General Rule

For most minor wounds with a standard adhesive island dressing:

**Change the dressing every 1–2 days**, or sooner if:
- It becomes wet or soaked through
- The edges start to peel or lift
- It looks dirty or has been contaminated
- There are signs of infection beneath it

Leaving a dressing on longer than 2 days without checking is generally not recommended for open wounds, even if it still looks intact from the outside.

## By Wound Type

### Minor cuts and abrasions
Once a clean, dry wound has been dressed, you can often leave it for 48 hours before the first change. Some very minor wounds (small, dry, superficial) can be left for up to 3 days if the dressing is clean and sealed.

### Surgical or post-operative wounds
Follow your surgeon's or nurse's specific instructions — these override any general guideline. Most post-op wound care protocols involve:
- First dressing change at 24–48 hours
- Subsequent changes every 2–3 days
- No bathing or soaking until wound is sealed

### Wounds with significant fluid (exudate)
Change more frequently — at least daily — or when the dressing pad is more than half saturated. A soaking dressing loses its bacterial barrier, putting the wound at risk.

### Infected wounds
If infection is present or suspected, more frequent dressing changes are required. Follow medical advice carefully and do not leave infected wounds unattended.

## Signs You Should Change the Dressing NOW

Don't wait for the scheduled change if you notice any of the following:

- **The dressing is wet through** — from wound fluid, sweat, or water
- **The edges have lifted** — exposing the wound to air and bacteria
- **You can see or smell discharge** — pus or an unpleasant odour under the dressing
- **Increased pain at the wound site** — can indicate infection or pressure
- **Redness or heat spreading from the edges** — signs of inflammation or infection

## Why You Shouldn't Change Dressings Too Often

There's a balance here that many people miss. Changing dressings more than once per day on a clean, healing wound can:

- Disturb the new tissue forming at the surface
- Introduce bacteria each time the wound is exposed
- Strip the surrounding skin, especially with adhesive dressings
- Slow healing by repeatedly removing the moist wound environment

Healing happens in the undisturbed space between dressing changes. The goal is to create a clean, stable environment and leave the wound alone to do its work.

## How to Change a Wound Dressing Safely

1. Wash hands thoroughly and put on clean gloves
2. Gently lift the edges of the old dressing — don't pull sharply
3. If the dressing is stuck, dampen it slightly with saline to loosen it before removal
4. Inspect the wound briefly — note any changes in colour, smell, or discharge
5. Clean the wound if needed (saline or cool running water)
6. Pat dry and apply a fresh sterile dressing

## When to Stop Using a Dressing

A wound no longer needs dressing once:
- The surface is fully closed (no gap or raw area visible)
- There is no more fluid being produced
- New skin has started to form over the area

At this point, leaving the wound to air — if hygiene permits — can help the final stages of healing. However, in areas prone to friction (e.g. under clothing, on a joint), continuing with a light dressing a little longer protects the new skin.

## Summary

| Dressing type | Standard change frequency |
|---|---|
| Adhesive island dressing (minor wound) | Every 1–2 days |
| Adhesive island dressing (surgical wound) | Per medical advice, typically every 2–3 days |
| Gauze or non-adhesive (exuding wound) | Every 24 hours or when saturated |
| Transparent film (superficial wound) | Every 3–5 days or when lifting |
